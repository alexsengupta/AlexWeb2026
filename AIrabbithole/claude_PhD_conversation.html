Reimagining the PhD in the Age of AI

This post is based on my opinion piece PhD training needs a reboot in an AI world
Alex Sen Gupta, University of New South Wales

Imagine if the conversation you're about to read were actually possible today. A PhD student and an AI system working together to complete months of research in just a few hours. What would that mean for doctoral programs? For how we train the next generation of researchers?

With the help of an AI, I created the semi-fictional dialogue (on the right) to explore these questions.

About this dialogue: I prompted an AI (Claude) to role play a smart-research capable AI of the future – HALe - with abilities that are in their infancy now, but are clearly developing rapidly (see the pre-prompt). I then played the role of a PhD student engaging HALe to help with a PhD project. The following is an unedited conversation with HALe (remember HALe is pretending to carry out these research tasks, not actually doing them).

PhDs will never be the same again

Since ChatGPT launched in late 2022, we've talked endlessly about preventing cheating in undergraduate courses, about whether students should be allowed to use these tools, about hallucinations and inaccuracies.

But these conversations were missing something crucial. While we fixated on preventing misuse in coursework, a deep transformation has also been quietly unfolding in research - in the work that defines what universities actually do.

To understand what this future might look like, I decided to try a thought experiment. I created a role-play scenario with Claude, one of the leading AI systems, imagining a hypothetical AI research assistant I called "HALe" (inspired by the infamous HAL 9000, but hopefully more cooperative).

The dialogue you're reading alongside this commentary is what emerged. It's fictional—but it's grounded in capabilities that either exist today or are clearly on the horizon.

What We're Watching

The research project in this simulation is deliberately ambitious: investigating how extreme ocean temperatures affect marine species. In a traditional PhD program, this would involve:

Deep dive What the traditional workflow demands

Without HALe, this same project would typically require:

  • Months of literature review to identify the state of knowledge
  • Weeks searching for and compiling relevant datasets
  • Extensive data cleaning and harmonization
  • Complex statistical analysis requiring specialized coding skills
  • Generating figures and other visualisations
  • Interpretation of results and comparison with existing literature
  • Writing, revision, and formatting for publication

Watch as this process unfolds in the conversation. Notice how HALe doesn't just assist—it takes initiative. It searches literature, identifies knowledge gaps, harmonizes data from different sources, runs statistical models, interprets results, makes important recommendations and even drafts a manuscript.

The entire workflow that would typically take a PhD student 4-6 months plays out in a conversation that might span a few hours.

The Speed That Unsettled Me

While creating this dialogue, I watched HALe systematically work through the research process: literature search - data integration - statistical modelling - result interpretation - manuscript drafting. It was like working with a hyper-competent research assistant who never got tired, never made careless errors, and could instantly recall and synthesize information from thousands of papers. The experience was both exhilarating and unsettling.

Just to be clear: today's AI can't actually do all of this autonomously. Not yet. But here's what struck me - every individual component of what HALe does in this conversation is either possible now with human guidance, or clearly under development.

Current AI models can draft credible literature reviews when given the right sources. They can write code that processes real datasets (which humans then need to validate). They can interpret statistical outputs and create visualizations. They can help structure and draft manuscripts.

We're not talking about some distant future. We're talking about capabilities emerging right now, improving month by month.

But Not All Research Is Like This

Before we get too carried away, there are limitations: this hypothetical project is computational and data-driven. It's the kind of research that's probably most vulnerable to AI acceleration.

If your PhD involves fieldwork—spending months collecting samples from remote locations, running delicate laboratory experiments, conducting interviews with people, or building physical prototypes—you're not going to be replaced by a chatbot anytime soon. The physical world still requires human presence and judgment.

But even in these fields, AI is creeping in. It can design experiments. It's synthesizing literature. It's analyzing results. Even the most hands-on PhD programs are going to feel this shift.

The Skills Paradox

Here's the rub: probably one of the most important skills we're going to need moving forward is to be able to spot where AIs are going wrong. But this requires deep understanding methods and procedures. You need to spot when the code is plausible but wrong. You need to recognize when a statistical approach is inappropriate. You need to understand if literature is being accurately portrayed. You need to know when an AI is confidently generating nonsense.

But these are precisely the skills that students have traditionally developed by doing the work themselves—by writing the code, running the analyses, deeply reading literature, making mistakes and learning from them.

If AI does the work, how do students develop the expertise needed to evaluate whether AI has done the work correctly?

This isn't just an academic puzzle. It's a genuine threat to the intellectual development of the next generation of researchers. We could end up with doctoral graduates who can orchestrate AI systems but who lack the deep understanding that comes from wrestling with problems themselves.

What Needs to Change

So what do we do? I don't think the answer is to ban AI from research—that's both impractical and would put our students at a massive disadvantage. But we need to fundamentally rethink doctoral training.

Here are some things I think need to happen:

Deep dive Four shifts doctoral programs must plan for

Redefine "original contribution": If AI can draft literature reviews and run standard analyses, then originality needs to mean something different. Maybe it's about asking novel questions, designing creative methodological approaches, or synthesizing insights across domains in unexpected ways.

Shift assessment approaches: A beautifully written thesis doesn't prove deep understanding anymore. We might need to rely more heavily on oral defenses, live problem-solving demonstrations, and reflective commentary on research decisions.

Teach critical AI evaluation: Students need to learn how to interrogate AI outputs, how to spot subtle errors, how to validate results using test datasets.

Reconsider program length and scope: If AI can compress months of work into days, do we shorten PhD programs? (I think this would be a very bad course of action). Or do we expand their ambition, tackling bigger, more interdisciplinary questions?

Stopping AI becoming an intellectual crutch: Students still need to get their hands dirty with data, to write code from scratch, to struggle with analysis—at least initially. We need to be deliberate about when we allow AI assistance and when we require students to work independently.

The Equity Question

Some universities are providing campus-wide access to advanced AI systems. Others can't afford to. Some students will have powerful AI assistants; others won't. We're potentially amplifying a two-tier system where some institutions and students are radically more productive than others, simply because of access to technology.

Why I'm Sharing This

I know many of my colleagues remain skeptical. They point to AI's current limitations: the hallucinations, the lack of true understanding, the inability to handle novel situations. And they're right that today's AI has all these problems.

But here's what worries me: these limitations are shrinking fast. The AI that was clumsy in 2023 is increasingly fluent in 2024 and 2025. The trajectory is clear.

I created this dialogue because I think we need to have these conversations now, while we still have time to shape how AI gets integrated into research training. Not in five years when it's already happened. Not when students are already dependent on tools we haven't figured out how to use responsibly.

The question isn't whether AI will transform doctoral education. The question is whether we'll actively shape that transformation or just let it happen to us.

An Invitation

If you're a PhD supervisor, I have a challenge for you: try this yourself. Take a research question in your field and put it to Claude, ChatGPT, or another advanced AI. Ask it to outline a methodology. Ask it to suggest analytical approaches. Watch it write code and interpret results.

I think you'll find the experience unsettling—not because the AI is perfect (it isn't), but because it's far more capable than you might expect. And it's improving at an incredible rate.

This dialogue isn't meant to be prescriptive. It's meant to provoke thought, to help us imagine what's coming, and to start the difficult conversations we need to have about how to prepare our students for a research landscape that's changing faster than our institutions.

Because here's the truth: AI isn't going away. The question is whether we'll shape its role in research training, or whether we'll let it shape us.

Title: "Universal thermal mortality metrics for Australia's marine habitat-forming species revealed through machine learning"

ABSTRACT
Marine heatwaves threaten habitat-forming species globally, yet optimal predictive metrics remain unclear and inconsistent across ecosystems. Here we apply machine learning to 2,341 documented mortality events across Australian coral reefs, kelp forests, seagrass meadows, and mangroves to identify optimal thermal stress predictors. While complex models incorporating 38-52 variables achieved exceptional predictive accuracy (R²=0.87-0.91), we demonstrate that simplified three-variable alternatives retain 90-95% of this performance. Remarkably, a single universal metric—combining temperature anomaly, duration, and rate of change—predicts mortality across all four habitat types with 79% accuracy, challenging the paradigm of habitat-specific monitoring. Validation across regions and time periods (2015-2024) confirms metric stability. These findings enable immediate implementation of improved monitoring systems while highlighting that most predictive power resides in a few key variables, not complex multi-parameter frameworks.

INTRODUCTION (Excerpt)
"Marine heatwaves have increased in frequency, intensity, and duration globally, with Australia experiencing some of the most severe events. The 2011 Western Australian heatwave caused unprecedented kelp forest loss, while the 2016 and 2017 thermal anomalies triggered consecutive mass coral bleaching across the Great Barrier Reef. Despite growing impacts, predictive frameworks remain fragmented—coral scientists rely on Degree Heating Weeks, kelp researchers focus on absolute temperature thresholds, while seagrass and mangrove studies lack standardized metrics entirely.

This methodological inconsistency prevents systematic cross-ecosystem risk assessment and hampers development of unified early warning systems. Here, we leverage machine learning to analyze the largest compilation of marine heatwave mortality events across Australia's four major habitat-forming groups..."

METHODS (Key Sections)
Data compilation and extraction: "We compiled mortality and severe stress events (>50% impact) from three sources: (1) structured databases including the National Coral Bleaching Database and IMOS biological records; (2) peer-reviewed literature tables; and (3) digitized data extracted from published figures using advanced graph extraction algorithms. This novel approach recovered 847 additional impact records (36% increase) from 186 paper figures that were previously unavailable for quantitative analysis."

Machine learning optimization: "We employed Random Forest regression with recursive feature elimination to identify optimal predictor combinations from >200 candidate metrics. Models were trained using spatially blocked cross-validation to prevent overfitting. To address implementation constraints, we systematically reduced model complexity, identifying minimal variable sets achieving >90% of maximum predictive performance..."

RESULTS (Sample Paragraphs)
"Machine learning models revealed strong habitat-specific divergence in optimal predictors (Fig. 1). Coral mortality was best predicted by a modified DHW incorporating rate of temperature increase (R²=0.91), while kelp loss showed strongest correlation with rate of warming above 2.5°C baseline (R²=0.89). Seagrass mortality aligned with duration of exposure above thermal thresholds (R²=0.88), and mangrove dieback correlated with cumulative exposure time during aerial exposure at low tide (R²=0.87).

However, the most striking finding emerged from our complexity reduction analysis. Three-variable simplified metrics achieved 83-86% accuracy for habitat-specific predictions—a mere 5-7% reduction from 38-52 variable complex models (Fig. 2). This pattern held across all habitats, suggesting that thermal mortality drivers, while varying between ecosystems, are fundamentally controlled by a small set of core processes.

The universal metric (Temperature Anomaly × Duration^0.5 × (1 + Rate of Change)) demonstrated remarkable consistency across habitats (R²=0.79) and regions (R²=0.77-0.81), with minimal degradation in temporal validation (0.78 for 2015-2024 events)..."

DISCUSSION (Key Points)
"Our findings challenge two paradigms in marine heatwave science. First, while machine learning confirms that habitat-specific metrics can achieve exceptional predictive power, the marginal gains beyond three-variable models are minimal—questioning whether implementation of complex monitoring frameworks is justified. Second, the strong performance of a universal metric suggests that fundamental thermal stress processes are more conserved across marine habitats than previously recognized.

The practical implications are immediate. Management agencies could implement our simplified habitat-specific metrics using existing infrastructure, achieving 83-86% predictive accuracy. Alternatively, the universal metric offers a single monitoring framework for all habitat types with 79% accuracy—potentially transforming how we approach ecosystem-wide marine heatwave warnings..."

PLAIN LANGUAGE SUMMARY (246 words):

Ocean heatwaves are becoming more frequent and intense, killing coral reefs, kelp forests, seagrasses, and mangroves that support marine life and protect coastlines. Currently, scientists use different temperature measurements to predict damage for each habitat type, making it difficult to provide consistent warnings.

We used artificial intelligence to analyze over 2,300 recorded die-off events around Australia, including data extracted from graphs in scientific papers that couldn't be analyzed before. The AI tested hundreds of temperature patterns to find what best predicts mortality.

Our findings suggest that despite their differences, coral reefs, kelp forests, seagrasses, and mangroves respond to heat stress in fundamentally similar ways. This universal pattern could transform how we monitor and protect marine ecosystems as climate change intensifies.
