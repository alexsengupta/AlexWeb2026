Key Findings: 
The study found that the interaction of SAM and ZW3 significantly influences sea ice variability, particularly in western Antarctica. During negative SAM conditions, for instance, stronger meridional (north-south) wind anomalies associated with ZW3 had a more pronounced effect on SIC in regions closer to the ice edge. The researchers noted that while SAM primarily affects sea ice through dynamic processes (e.g., the advection of ice), ZW3 has distinct impacts based on its phase, which leads to different SIC responses. 

For example, they discovered that during positive SAM events, sea ice concentration tended to increase due to stronger westerly winds that pushed ice equatorward. Conversely, during negative SAM events, the dynamic effects of ZW3-associated winds could lead to significant decreases in sea ice concentration. The study also highlighted that regions with a prominent angle of the ice edge relative to wind direction experienced localized changes in SIC, indicating that the orientation of the ice edge plays a critical role in how wind affects sea ice.
