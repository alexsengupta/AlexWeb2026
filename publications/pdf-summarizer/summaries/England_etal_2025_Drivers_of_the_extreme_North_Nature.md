### Key Findings

The researchers discovered that the marine heatwave was primarily driven by significantly reduced wind speeds. In June 2023, the average wind speed over the eastern North Atlantic reached unprecedented lows, leading to a phenomenon known as "mixed layer shoaling." This means that the upper layer of the ocean became shallower than usual, allowing for rapid warming.

Specifically, they noted that by mid-summer, parts of the North Atlantic were experiencing sea surface temperatures (SST) that were more than 2°C above the average for the period of 1981-2010. The eastern North Atlantic saw initial warming in June, followed by a surge in the western region during July. This pattern of warming was not just a statistical anomaly; it was associated with severe weather events in Europe, including intense heatwaves and heavy rainfall.

The researchers quantified the warming and found that the area-averaged mixed layer depth during the summer was about 20% shallower than the historical average, revealing a significant trend towards shallower mixed layers over the past few decades. This shallow mixed layer means that less energy is required to heat the surface layer, leading to rapid temperature increases.
