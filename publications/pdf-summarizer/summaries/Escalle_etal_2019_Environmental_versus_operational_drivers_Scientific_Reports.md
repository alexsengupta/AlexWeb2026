The study aims to understand the factors that contribute to dFAD beaching events, which have been identified as a significant environmental concern due to their potential to pollute the ocean and harm marine life.

The researchers undertook a comprehensive analysis to estimate the frequency and distribution of dFAD beaching events in the WCPO, specifically focusing on data collected from 2016 to 2017. They tracked over 22,000 dFADs and conducted Lagrangian simulations of virtual dFADs to explore both environmental factors (like ocean currents) and operational factors (such as how and where dFADs are deployed) that influence beaching occurrences. Interestingly, in Tuvalu, beaching rates were closely linked to the density of deployed dFADs, indicating a different set of factors at play. The results reveal that addressing the issue of dFAD beaching will require a multifaceted approach. For example, simply changing where dFADs are deployed may not significantly reduce beaching occurrences in areas heavily influenced by ocean currents. Instead, the researchers recommend a combination of strategies, including limiting the number of dFADs deployed, employing biodegradable materials for dFAD construction, and establishing recovery initiatives for dFADs before they reach sensitive coastal areas.
