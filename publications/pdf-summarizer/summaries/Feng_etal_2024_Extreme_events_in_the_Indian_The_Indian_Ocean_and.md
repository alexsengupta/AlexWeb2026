The Indian Ocean is home to about one-third of the world's population, with many countries having low-lying coastal areas that are particularly vulnerable due to high population density and limited disaster management capabilities. For instance, the devastating marine heatwave off the western coast of Australia in 2011 led to dramatic shifts in marine ecosystems, while the Bay of Bengal has seen some of the deadliest tropical cyclones, resulting in hundreds of thousands of fatalities over the decades. This research aims to explore the dynamics of these extreme events and their impacts, particularly concerning climate variability and change.

The researchers conducted a comprehensive review of existing literature and data concerning MHWs, TCs, and tsunamis in the Indian Ocean. Key Findings:

The study revealed several crucial findings. First, MHWs have become more frequent and intense in the Indian Ocean, with a documented increase of over 50% in annual MHW days globally over the last century, largely attributed to anthropogenic climate change. Notably, the 2008 Cyclone Nargis exemplified this trend, resulting in over 130,000 deaths and significant economic loss.

Lastly, tsunamis, while less frequent than in the Pacific, have had catastrophic impacts in the Indian Ocean. The 2004 tsunami, which followed a massive underwater earthquake, resulted in approximately 230,000 fatalities across multiple countries. The study highlighted the need for robust tsunami warning systems and improved preparedness to mitigate future risks.
