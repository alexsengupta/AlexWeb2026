## Detailed Briefing (800-1000 words)

The research presented in the article addresses a critical question in climate science: how does the El Niño Southern Oscillation (ENSO) respond to different warming scenarios, particularly during the Mid-Pliocene Warm Period (mPWP), around three million years ago? The mPWP is significant because it was a time when global temperatures were similar to those projected for the near future due to rising carbon dioxide levels. Understanding ENSO's behavior in this historical context could provide vital insights into its future dynamics as the Earth continues to warm.

The researchers conducted a comprehensive analysis using a suite of climate models from the Pliocene Model Intercomparison Project (PlioMIP). Their approach involved simulating the climatic conditions of the mPWP and examining how shifts in the Inter-Tropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) influenced ENSO activity. The ITCZ is a region near the equator where trade winds from the Northern and Southern Hemispheres converge, leading to significant weather patterns. By studying the relationship between the ITCZ's position and ENSO activity, the researchers aimed to uncover the mechanisms behind the observed reduction in ENSO variability during this warm period.

The findings revealed that during the mPWP, there was a substantial reduction in the amplitude of ENSO events, with models showing an average decrease of 25% in variability. This decrease was not solely due to changes in the equatorial Pacific's mean state, but rather linked to a northward shift of the ITCZ. The researchers noted that this shift resulted in intensified southeast trade winds and a stronger South Pacific Subtropical High, both of which suppressed conditions favorable for ENSO development. Essentially, a warmer climate with a shifted ITCZ led to a more stable atmosphere and ocean system, making extreme ENSO events less likely.

One of the most striking aspects of the research is its implication for future climate predictions. The results suggest that if current trends continue, we might see an increase in ENSO variability as the ITCZ shifts southward, a scenario projected for the 21st century. This is contrary to the mPWP scenario, where warming led to a suppression of ENSO activity. Such findings highlight the complex interplay between climate dynamics and atmospheric circulation patterns, suggesting that while warmer temperatures can stabilize certain systems, they can also lead to increased variability in others, depending on geographic and atmospheric changes.

The implications of this research are vast. ENSO significantly influences global weather patterns, affecting agriculture, fisheries, and water supply. A reduction in ENSO variability could lead to more predictable weather patterns, which might benefit certain agricultural practices. Conversely, if future warming leads to increased ENSO activity, this could exacerbate extreme weather events, such as droughts or floods, with profound impacts on economies and ecosystems worldwide.

In summary, the study emphasizes the importance of understanding past climate conditions to inform future predictions. The northward shift of the ITCZ during the mPWP offers a critical lesson: that changes in climate can have counterintuitive effects on weather patterns.
