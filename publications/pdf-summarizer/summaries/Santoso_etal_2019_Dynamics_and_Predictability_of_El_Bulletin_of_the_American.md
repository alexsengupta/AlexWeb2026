### Background/Context

ENSO consists of two main phases: El Niño, characterized by warmer ocean temperatures in the central and eastern Pacific, and La Niña, marked by cooler temperatures. These phases can lead to drastic weather changes. For instance, El Niño years are often associated with increased temperatures and severe droughts in Australia, while La Niña years can bring heavy rainfall and flooding. The Australian Bureau of Meteorology has been issuing ENSO outlooks since 2000 to help prepare for these events, but predicting their impacts remains a challenge due to the complexity of ENSO dynamics.

The variability of ENSO impacts is well-documented, but the article highlights specific events like the 1997/98 and 2002/03 El Niños, which had very different effects despite their intensities. The moderate 2002/03 El Niño caused a notable 25% drop in agricultural output due to severe drought, while the extreme 1997/98 event had a more localized impact. This inconsistency emphasizes the need for improved understanding and forecasting of ENSO events.

They argue that the spatial structures of these events are crucial for predicting their impacts.
