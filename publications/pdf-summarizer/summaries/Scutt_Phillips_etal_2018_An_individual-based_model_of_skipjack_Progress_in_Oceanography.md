Background/Context

The research focuses on skipjack tuna (Katsuwonus pelamis), a highly valued fish species that ranks as the third most exploited fish globally. Approximately 70% of the estimated three million tons of skipjack tuna caught annually come from the Pacific Ocean. While current assessments indicate that Pacific stocks are not overfished, there is growing concern about the uncertainty surrounding the spatial parameters of stock assessment models, particularly regarding the movement rates of these fish. Understanding the distribution and movement patterns of skipjack tuna is vital for sustainable fisheries management, especially given that these fish are influenced by a range of ecological interactions and environmental factors, including temperature and ocean currents.

Traditional models used in fisheries science, particularly Eulerian models, analyze fish populations at fixed locations, which can miss critical individual-level behaviors and movement dynamics. This research aims to address this gap by introducing a new individual-based model (IBM) called Ikamoana. This model simulates the movements of individual skipjack tuna and aims to provide a more nuanced understanding of their distribution and connectivity between different regions, which is crucial for more accurate fisheries assessments.

Main Research/Argument

The researchers developed the Ikamoana model as a Lagrangian approach, which tracks individual tuna movements through space rather than relying on fixed spatial densities. This model simulates the movement of skipjack tuna based on physical ocean currents, habitat-dependent behaviors (kinesis), and directed movements (taxis). By applying Ikamoana to the same data used in the existing Eulerian model, SEAPODYM, the researchers aimed to replicate the density distribution of skipjack tuna while also exploring how individual trajectories can provide insights into connectivity between different regions.

The model was parameterized using data from two contrasting temporal scenarios: a neutral period (2003-2007) and a period with significant El Niño and La Niña events (1997-2001). By comparing the results from Ikamoana with SEAPODYM, the researchers intended to validate the new model and demonstrate its effectiveness in tracking the movement of individual schools of tuna.

Key Findings

The results indicated that the Ikamoana model accurately replicated the density distribution of skipjack tuna as predicted by SEAPODYM, achieving a spatial correlation of over 0.96 at all times. The researchers found that retention rates in various assessment regions were significantly lower—by as much as 50%—than previously estimated by SEAPODYM. This suggests that assuming fixed transfer rates between regions can lead to erroneous stock estimates, particularly in light of changing ocean conditions such as those associated with El Niño or La Niña events.

The model also revealed that skipjack tuna exhibited greater connectivity between regions than previously understood. For example, during the El Niño period, the model showed increased movement of tuna from the western warm pool region to the central Pacific, indicating that environmental factors significantly influence tuna distribution. Furthermore, the individual trajectories tracked by Ikamoana allowed the researchers to observe how fish moved between regions, often taking routes through other areas not accounted for in traditional models.
