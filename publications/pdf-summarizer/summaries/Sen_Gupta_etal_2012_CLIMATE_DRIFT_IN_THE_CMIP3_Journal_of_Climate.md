## Detailed Briefing (800-1000 words)

Climate models are essential for understanding and predicting long-term changes in the Earth's climate. However, these models often exhibit a phenomenon known as "climate drift," which can lead to inaccurate projections. This article by Sen Gupta et al. focuses on quantifying climate drift in the Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 3 (CMIP3) models, which were used to inform the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Fourth Assessment Report. By addressing the issue of climate drift, the researchers aim to provide clarity on how it affects climate simulations and the implications for climate change projections.

Climate drift refers to long-term trends in climate model simulations that occur even without any external influences, such as greenhouse gas emissions. This drift can be caused by several factors, including the way different parts of the model interact with each other, deficiencies in the model's physics, and inaccuracies in how the model is initialized. For instance, when coupling ocean and atmospheric models, rapid adjustments can introduce errors that persist over time. Understanding and addressing these drifts is crucial because they can significantly affect historical and future climate trend analyses.

To quantify climate drift, the authors analyzed simulations from the CMIP3 models, focusing on trends from 1950 to 2000. The researchers employed a method where they compared control simulations—runs without external forcing—with forced simulations that included historical climate data. By studying these two simulation types, the authors aimed to identify and measure the drift's impact on various climate properties.

The findings revealed that climate drift is generally smaller than the observed and modeled trends in global surface properties, but it can still introduce significant errors, especially at local scales. For example, drift in sea surface temperature (SST) and surface air temperature (SAT) during the 50-year trend was found to account for less than 20% of the total warming signal in most models. However, some individual models exhibited drift magnitudes that exceeded 30% of the forced trends, indicating that drift can substantially alter the interpretation of local changes.

One striking result was that drift tends to dominate trends in the deep ocean. Below depths of 1,000 to 2,000 meters, drift often overshadows any forced trends, which could lead to misleading conclusions about ocean heat content and sea level rise. For example, the study noted that in some regions, the direction of sea-level trends could be reversed when drift is accounted for, highlighting the importance of considering drift when interpreting oceanic data.

The significance of these findings extends beyond academic interest. As climate models inform policymakers and stakeholders about future climate scenarios, understanding drift is crucial for accurate assessments. For instance, localized climate predictions—such as those affecting specific regions or smaller areas—could be significantly impacted by drift, potentially leading to erroneous conclusions about climate risks and the need for adaptation strategies.

Furthermore, the research underscores the need for improved model initialization and calibration to mitigate drift. While the article acknowledges that climate drift is an enduring issue, it suggests that improved model physics and higher resolutions may help reduce its impacts in future climate projections. For example, the study highlights a trend where newer models are less reliant on flux adjustments, leading to better representation of observed climate conditions.

One broader takeaway from this research is the reminder that while models are powerful tools for climate prediction, they are not infallible. The presence of drift serves as a cautionary signal about the complexities of modeling climate systems. It emphasizes the importance of continuous improvement in modeling techniques and the need for transparency in how models are initialized and run. Policymakers and scientists alike must remain vigilant about these issues, as they directly affect the robustness of climate projections and the strategies developed to address climate change.
