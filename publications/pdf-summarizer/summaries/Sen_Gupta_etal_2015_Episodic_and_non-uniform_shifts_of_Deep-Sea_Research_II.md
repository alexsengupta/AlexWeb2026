### Background/Context
The oceans have been experiencing increased temperatures over the past century, and this trend is expected to accelerate. This warming poses significant threats to marine biodiversity, as many marine species are particularly sensitive to temperature changes. For instance, species that thrive in specific thermal ranges must either adapt, migrate to cooler waters, or face the risk of extinction. With historical observations and projections indicating widespread poleward shifts of thermal habitats, this research addresses the pressing question of how quickly and uniformly these shifts are occurring across different ocean regions.

The motivation for this study is underscored by the increasing evidence of shifts in marine species distributions, which have already led to significant ecological and economic impacts. For example, as waters warm, commercially important fish species are moving towards the poles, altering fishing patterns and threatening the livelihoods of communities dependent on these resources.

### Main Research/Argument
The researchers employed a two-pronged approach: analyzing historical ocean temperature data and utilizing climate model projections to assess isotherm movements. They focused on how isotherm speeds—the rates at which the boundaries of thermal habitats are shifting—differ across various ocean regions and time scales.

To understand isotherm movement, the researchers used a mathematical formula that relates local sea surface temperature (SST) changes to meridional temperature gradients. This formula allows them to estimate how quickly isotherms might move based on local warming trends and geographical variations.

For instance, they found that current isotherm speeds are about 13 kilometers per decade on average, but this rate can vary significantly depending on factors like the strength of local temperature gradients and specific oceanic conditions. They also highlighted that isotherm movements are not always gradual; they can be erratic, stalling at thermal fronts for extended periods before suddenly shifting to new positions.

One of the most significant findings is the predicted acceleration of isotherm speeds in the 21st century. Under a "business as usual" emissions scenario (RCP85), median isotherm speeds are projected to be approximately 70 kilometers per decade, which is around seven times faster than historical rates. This is particularly relevant for species in the northern hemisphere during summer months, where the combination of heightened warming and weaker temperature gradients leads to faster shifts.

Furthermore, the researchers noted that extreme temperature events—such as marine heatwaves—can cause sudden and localized extinctions, which add another layer of complexity to marine species' responses to climate change. For example, a significant heatwave in Western Australia resulted in the temporary extinction of habitat-forming algal species.
