In Australia, particularly in Queensland, the intense rainfall led to severe flooding, claiming 35 lives and costing billions in damages. The rainfall was 84% above average for that time of year, marking it as exceptionally unusual, even for a strong La Niña. The researchers aimed to dissect the mechanisms behind the extreme rainfall during this period, particularly focusing on how long-term ocean warming might have contributed to these conditions. They set out to answer a critical question: how does a warmer ocean state impact atmospheric conditions that lead to heavy rainfall?

**Main Research/Argument**  
To tackle this question, the researchers employed atmospheric general circulation models (AGCM) to simulate conditions during the 2010/2011 La Niña event. They ran experiments comparing ocean conditions with and without the long-term warming trend. This approach is akin to a controlled experiment in a laboratory, where one variable is changed while all others are kept constant to observe the effects.

The models used data from various sources, including observed sea surface temperatures (SST), to understand how changes in ocean temperatures influenced atmospheric moisture transport and precipitation patterns. The research involved rigorous statistical testing to identify significant anomalies in rainfall and soil moisture compared to long-term averages.

**Key Findings**  
The results of the simulations were striking. The study found that the likelihood of extreme rainfall in northeast Australia was significantly increased due to the warmer ocean conditions. For example, the average precipitation for the September-February period during the 2010/2011 event reached 136 mm/month, a stark increase compared to the long-term average of 74 mm/month. The study also highlighted that the extreme moisture convergence and upward motion over eastern Australia were directly linked to these warmer SSTs, creating conditions conducive to heavy rainfall.
