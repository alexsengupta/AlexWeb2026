Background/Context

Marine heatwaves (MHWs) are becoming increasingly prevalent and severe due to climate change, making them a critical issue for marine ecosystems and human societies. These events are characterized by periods when ocean temperatures rise significantly above average, often resulting in devastating impacts on marine biodiversity and the ecosystem services that humans rely on. The urgency of this issue has been underscored by recent alarming trends: recent years have seen a dramatic increase in both the frequency and intensity of MHWs, with 2023 and 2024 marking new historical highs. The consequences are vast, affecting everything from fish populations to coral reefs and the livelihoods of communities dependent on marine resources.

The researchers conducted a thorough review to assess the impacts of MHWs on marine organisms and the services they provide. The findings from this research are significant for both ecological and socio-economic spheres. MHWs are not just a scientific curiosity; they pose real threats to marine ecosystems and the communities that depend on them. This loss can lead to diminished fisheries, disrupted food webs, and reduced carbon storage capacities, ultimately affecting global food security and climate regulation.

Additionally, coral bleaching events linked to MHWs have not only affected local biodiversity but have also led to significant economic declines in tourism, particularly in regions reliant on healthy coral reefs for recreational activities.

2. **Understanding Complex Interactions**: Human-induced changes have created complex interactions among species that complicate predictions related to MHWs. For instance, the introduction of invasive species and changes in nutrient dynamics can exacerbate the effects of MHWs.
